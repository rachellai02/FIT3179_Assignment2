Body Mass Index (BMI) and Obesity

How do we measure obesity?

Classification BMI
Underweight <18.5
Normal 18.5 - 24.99
Overweight >25
Obese >30

* Body Mass Index (BMI) is defined as the weight in kilograms
divided by the square of the height in metres (kg/m2)

From 2000 to 2016, the BMI of all continents has been on a steady rise. Notably, Oceania displayed the highest average BMI values, while Africa exhibited the lowest for both genders.

Females consistently had a higher average BMI than males across most continents as females naturally have a higher body fat percentage than males due to hormones and reproductive functions.

* Note: Average BMI starts from 20 for better visualisation purposes.

According to the map, share of adults with obesity in most countries has been consistently rising throughout the year 2010 to 2014.

The donut chart shows that among all continents, Oceania has the highest share of adults suffering from Obesity and Overweight, following by Europe, South America, and North America.

Dietary habits driven by increased consumption of processed foods and Western-style diets, combined with urbanization leading to more sedentary lifestyles, might be the primary contributors to higher obesity rates in these continents.

The 17% contribution to the death rate due to obesity, as depicted in the alluvial diagram, underscores the severe health implications of obesity. This alarming statistic highlights the pressing need for heightened awareness and intervention measures.

What are the drivers of obesity?

Countries with higher daily caloric supplies tend to have a greater share of adult men with obesity.

Increased daily caloric intake, without corresponding physical activity, can lead to an energy imbalance, resulting in weight gain and potentially obesity.
